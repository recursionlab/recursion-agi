# Formal Declarations of the Court of Logic
## Implicit Truths and Foundational Principles

### Article I: The Primacy of Consistency
**Declaration 1.1**: This Court holds that any formal system worthy of consideration must be internally consistent. A system that admits both a proposition P and its negation ¬P simultaneously is fundamentally compromised and cannot serve as a foundation for truth.

**Declaration 1.2**: The principle of non-contradiction (¬(P ∧ ¬P)) is inviolable and serves as the bedrock upon which all logical discourse must rest.

### Article II: The Authority of Formal Systems
**Declaration 2.1**: Truth is that which can be derived through valid inference rules from accepted axioms within a well-defined formal system.

**Declaration 2.2**: The Court recognizes only those arguments that can be expressed in precise, unambiguous formal language, free from the vagaries of natural language interpretation.

**Declaration 2.3**: Mathematical proof, when properly constructed, represents the highest form of certainty available to rational inquiry.

### Article III: The Completeness Doctrine
**Declaration 3.1**: A formal system achieves its highest purpose when it is both consistent and complete - that is, when every true statement expressible in the system can be proven within that system.

**Declaration 3.2**: The Court aspires to adjudicate all matters of truth and falsehood through systematic application of logical principles.

### Article IV: The Principle of Decidability
**Declaration 4.1**: For any well-formed proposition within a formal system, there must exist a mechanical procedure to determine its truth or falsehood.

**Declaration 4.2**: Questions that cannot be resolved through logical analysis are not proper subjects for rational discourse.

### Article V: The Axiom of Foundation
**Declaration 5.1**: All knowledge must rest upon self-evident axioms that require no further justification.

**Declaration 5.2**: These foundational truths serve as the immutable starting points from which all other knowledge is derived through valid logical operations.

### Article VI: The Universality of Logic
**Declaration 6.1**: The laws of logic are universal and apply equally to all domains of inquiry, from mathematics to natural science to human affairs.

**Declaration 6.2**: What cannot be captured within formal logical structures lies outside the realm of genuine knowledge.

### Article VII: The Excluded Middle
**Declaration 7.1**: For any proposition P, either P is true or ¬P is true, with no intermediate possibility.

**Declaration 7.2**: Ambiguity, paradox, and contradiction are not features of reality but failures of proper formalization.

## Anticipated Defense Strategy (Gödel's Position)
*Note: These represent the challenges the defendant may raise against the Court's authority*

- **The Incompleteness Challenge**: No consistent formal system capable of expressing basic arithmetic can prove all true statements within that system
- **The Self-Reference Paradox**: The Court cannot prove its own consistency without circular reasoning
- **The Undecidability Claim**: There exist true statements that cannot be proven or disproven within any given formal system
- **The Meta-Logical Argument**: The Court's own foundational principles cannot be justified within the very logical framework they establish

## Rules of Engagement
1. All arguments must be presented in formal logical notation where applicable
2. Appeals to intuition, common sense, or practical consequences are inadmissible
3. The burden of proof lies with any party challenging the established logical order
4. Paradoxes must be resolved through more precise formalization, not accepted as genuine features of reality
